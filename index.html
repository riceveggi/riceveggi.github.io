INTRODUCTION¶

As the Internet improves connectivity around the world every day, there seems to be an endless amount of fresh art, film and other media in the world.

Thanks to the Internet, K Pop, an acronym for Korean popular music, was introduced to many countries and cultures around the globe. Anyone who has seen the Gangnam Style' music video might think that they know a bit about K-Pop, but K-Pop is more than just this song.

According to Vox, South Korean artists have charted the Billboard Hot 100 at least eight times since the Wonder Girls first entered with their hit "Nobody", released in four different languages including English in 2009. Since then, K-pop exports have grown significantly, reaching $189.7 million only in music album exports in 2021.

Now that we know what and how influential K-Pop is, the next question would be: who are K-Pop audiences? Analyzing the characteristics of K pop fans, who are responsible for the enormous sales of the billion dollar industry, can help ad companies gain deeper understanding of their current and potential customers to improve marketing strategy, customer experience, and brand perception. Doing so can promote an increase in revenues and net profit for not only the Korean music companies, but also ad companies and social media apps such as Facebook.

In this tutorial, our goal is to guide the readers through the five stages of Data Science Pipeline: Data collection, Data management, Exploratory data analysis, Hypothesis testing, and Communication of insights attained. Which in our case will be collecting K-pop survey data gathered from social media, cleaning and tidying survey answers, performing demographic analysis of respondents vs money spent on K-pop, predicting how much money is spent given demographic characteristics, and finally conclude and discuss possible further exploration regarding different ways to explore the K-pop industry.

1. DATA COLLECTION:¶

The dataset was obtained from this link. Survey data was collected from 240 respondents between February 2019 and May 2019. 23 different questions were asked, and the response type for each question differed as well. For example, 5 questions only asked Yes or No, while most of the others were multiple choice, or even multiple choice and open-ended (meaning respondents can give feedback through a text box) type questions.

We converted the downloaded xlsx file to csv to make it easier to read and convert to dataframe. pd.read_csv() was a useful function to convert the csv file to dataframe. When reading through the data entries of the table, we noticed some repeated non-ascii characters such as ''. The character was found in place of where an apostrophe should be so we replaced it by doing str.replace('','\''). Now that the data table has been somewhat readable, lets move on to the next step: data cleaning.

2. DATA CLEANING¶

Because of how the survey was designed, it took some time to figure out how to tidy and clean the data when graphing different plots when performing exploratory data analysis and hypothesis testing. Although none of the responses were missing, we had to figure out whether to include or exclude some survey answers. And even though we decide to drop the response, that doesnt mean we could disregard the entire data entry, or row in this case, of the table because the same table row may have appropriate responses for other types of questions, or columns in this case.

So we decided to split the dataframe into multiple dataframes for exploratory analysis so that we could drop rows based on the plots and analyzawe've created and made in the third part of this tutorial without affecting other possible analyses we could perform on.

Before doing cleaning, we dropped some columns that we deemed irrelevant to the goal of this tutorial and decided to include the following questions: Which is your favorite K-Pop group?

  • On an average, in one year, how much do you spend on K-Pop merchandise?
  • How old are you?
  • What is your gender?
  • When did you start listening to K-Pop?
  • If you do watch K-Pop music videos, how long do you spend watching them?
  • What do you do to keep up with K-Pop news?
  • Do you attend K-Pop concerts?
  • What is your profession?
  • Would you say that you are obsessed with K-Pop?
  • How many hours do you spend listening to K-Pop?

We then renamed the remaining columns for readability.

In our data exploratory analysis stage, we will examine the relationship between the responses of these columns as we plot different types of graphs. Before splitting the dataframes, we renamed the columns to make it easier to read.

First we cleaned the MONEY_SPENT column by replacing and separating all data into five categories: 0, less than \$50, \$50 to \$100, \$100 to \$200, \$200 to \$300, \$2000 or more, \$5000 or more and Unknown. We made a separate category for unknown for some open-ended responses because it was not possible to speculate the appropriate category unlike other open-ended responses. For example, answers such as 10? or "I spend less than \$50 cuz I'm broke, but if I had money, I would probably spend \$200+" was easy to classify as less than $50 compared to other responses such as "Depends but usually not a lot. ". Because it was difficult to assume the category, and if we did, it might have affected the data plots and integrity of the dataset. Therefore we decided to remove the unknown values from the dataset because we cannot connect the responses to other columns.

We then created a new column MONEY_SPENT_INT to convert the cleaned MONEY_SPENT column to integer values. To do so, we took the average of each range of the responses. For example 50 to 100 was replaced with 75, 100 to 200 was replaced with 150 and so on. This was a necessary step for us to plot the graphs to discover the relationship between other responses.

We have performed the same replacing, cleaning, and conversion for other columns such as hours_listened, years_listened, and hours_watched. For the k-pop_news column, we counted the number of types of k-pop news and made a separate new column num_suscribed. After most of the cleaning was done, we dropped unnecessary columns.

Conclusion and Communications and Insights Attained¶

The low accuracy and precision of the LDA model made us realize that the data was not consistent for the machine learning model. We speculate that the issue was due to not having enough data to correlate the different variables. Before cleaning, the dataset initially had 240 entries but after removing rows that were impossible to categorize, it was less than 220. In addition, even if we were trying to be as objective as possible when removing data, it is impossible to ignore the fact that our data selection was biased at the slightest. If we have performed the same tutorial for dataset size at least 1000 entries, we are certain that the LDA model would have given us better numbers.

That aside, we still predicted average money spent vs (average hours listened, average hours watched, and average years listened) using a linear regression model, which gave us positive slopes.
